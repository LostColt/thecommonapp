The Six Moves

These aren't tricks. They're tools for reflection — specific ways to pause the story and reveal something about yourself that pure narration never could.

Each move asks you to stop pushing forward and instead look inward:

  • What did this moment make me wonder?
  • How have I changed since then?
  • What did this cost me?
  • What contradictions am I holding?

Use them when your essay feels like it's telling but not showing. Use them when you've narrated the whole story but the reader still doesn't know who you are.

The slides below break down each move with examples you can apply to your own writing.

The Problem
Narration Is Great. It's Also the Problem.

Storytelling is powerful. It pulls readers in. It creates momentum.

But momentum can become a trap.

When you're caught in the current of your own story, you push forward — event after event, detail after detail — when the essay actually needs you to slow down.

The college essay isn't about what happened. It's about what you understood.

These six moves are interruptions. They force you to pause, reflect, and show the reader who you are beneath the story.

Move 1
The Interrupt Move

Action: Pause the narrative to ask the question the moment raises.

Instead of rushing to the next event, stop. Ask yourself what this moment made you wonder. Then write that question into your essay.

Example
"Why did this matter more than I expected?"
Move 2
The Then/Now Move

Action: Contrast what you thought then with what you understand now.

This move shows growth without stating it. The gap between "then" and "now" reveals how you've changed.

Example
"Back then I thought winning was everything. Now I see it was about something else entirely."
Move 3
The Zoom-Out Move

Action: Connect the moment to a larger pattern in your life.

One story becomes meaningful when it's part of something bigger. Show readers this isn't isolated — it's who you are.

Example
"This wasn't the first time I'd found myself on the outside looking in."
Move 4
The Cost Move

Action: Name what you gained AND what you lost.

Real growth has trade-offs. Acknowledging the cost makes your insight believable and mature.

Example
"I earned their respect. I also lost something I can't quite name — maybe the freedom to fail quietly."
Move 5
The Contradiction Move

Action: Hold two conflicting truths at once.

Life is messy. The best essays don't resolve everything neatly — they sit in the complexity.

Example
"I felt proud of what I'd done. I also felt uncomfortable with how I'd done it."
Move 6
The Sensory-Anchor Move

Action: Return to a concrete detail that carries meaning.

Abstract ideas float away. A specific image — a sound, a texture, a smell — makes your insight land.

Example
"Whenever I think about that summer, I still hear the screen door slamming behind me."
